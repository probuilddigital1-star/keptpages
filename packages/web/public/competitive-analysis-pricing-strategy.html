04 — Consumer Behavior

Why Subscription Pricing Fails This Customer

Karen's project takes 3 weeks. A subscription asks her to pay for 52.

No Ongoing Need

Karen scans Grandma's recipes in November, orders the book for Christmas, and never opens the app again. The $40 renewal email next November feels like a ripoff.

Commitment Anxiety

For a 58-year-old burned by forgotten subscriptions, "free trial → auto-renew" triggers instant skepticism. She'll Google "KeptPages cancel" before signing up.

One-Time Psychology

When cleaning out Mom's house, Karen thinks "how much does this cost" — not "how much per month." The mental model is purchasing, not subscribing.

Structural 100% Churn

If the average customer completes their project in 2–4 weeks, monthly churn approaches 100%. Annual renewal approaches 0%. Acquisition cost is wasted on customers who will never renew.

Wrong Comparison Set

Karen compares you to "I could just take photos and make a Shutterfly book for $30." The subscription makes you look more expensive, not less.

Book = Marketing

Every printed book sitting on a kitchen counter is an ad. A subscription wall before the book purchase reduces books in the world — which reduces your word-of-mouth engine.

The Core Tension

The subscription model optimizes for recurring revenue from power users. But KeptPages' best customers are one-time buyers with high order values and powerful referral potential. The pricing model should match the customer, not the other way around.

Why One-Time, Not Subscription

$59 one-time feels like "buying the full version" — a familiar software purchasing pattern for Boomers and Gen X. No cancellation anxiety. No renewal surprise.

The math still works: Even a power user doing 500 scans costs us ~$0.10 in API calls. The $59 is nearly pure margin.

The 15% print discount creates ongoing revenue without subscription friction. Keeper Pass pays for itself on the first book order (~$10 saved on a Premium book).

When to offer it: Post-purchase confirmation email. "Loved making this book? Get 15% off your next one forever." Don't upsell during the emotional design moment.

09 — Growth Multiplier

The Book Is the Marketing

No add-ons, no gimmicks. The physical book sitting on a kitchen counter is the most powerful referral engine we can build.

The Moment

The Kitchen Counter Effect

Karen's sister visits for Thanksgiving. She picks up the book, flips through it, recognizes Mom's handwriting. She asks: "How did you make this?" Karen says "KeptPages." That's the referral. No QR code required.

Built Into the Book

Colophon Branding

A tasteful "Made with KeptPages.com" on the copyright page — the same way Shutterfly and Blurb brand their photo books. Subtle, professional, and always there when someone flips to the front.

User-Created

Dedication Pages

Karen already has the tools to add a personal dedication page in the book designer. No need to charge for what she can do herself — and the emotional dedication page is what makes the book feel like a gift, not a product.

The Referral Math

Karen orders 4 books for her siblings. Each sibling shows their copy to friends and family. If just 1 in 4 recipients tells someone who becomes a customer, that's ~1 new book-ordering customer per original order — pure word-of-mouth with zero ad spend. Multi-copy discounts (15% off at 3+ copies) encourage larger orders, which means more books in the world, which means more referrals.

Why Not Paid Add-Ons?

We evaluated gift wrapping, gift message inserts, and branded QR cards. All three failed the reality check:

Add-On Problem
Gift wrapping Lulu (our print partner) doesn't support it. Would require a separate fulfillment partner.
Gift message insert Customers can already add a dedication page in the book designer for free. Charging for it feels dishonest.
Branded QR card Lulu can't insert physical cards. Printing it as a page in the book puts an ad in someone's family heirloom. Wrong tone for this product.
10 — Friction Elimination

Remove Every Barrier Between Karen and Her Book

The customer journey should feel like making the book, not like configuring software.

  1. Let her scan before creating an account.
    The AI extraction is the aha moment. Let Karen scan 3–5 recipes before asking for an email. Prove the magic first.
  2. Show the book preview early.
    Even with 5 scans, auto-generate a preview of what the book could look like. Make her imagine holding it. This is what converts browsers to buyers.
  3. Name book tiers emotionally, not technically.
    "Classic / Premium / Heirloom" instead of "Softcover B&W 60# uncoated." Karen doesn't speak print jargon. She speaks gift-giving.
  4. Make multi-copy ordering dead simple.
    "How many copies?" with a quantity selector and automatic discount display. Not a separate flow. Not a coupon code. One click.
  5. Show shipping estimates on the book preview page.
    "Order today, arrives by [date]" removes the "Will it get here by Christmas?" anxiety that kills conversions in Q4.
  6. Don't upsell during the emotional moment.
    When Karen is choosing her cover photo and tearing up, don't pitch the Keeper Pass. Let her buy the book. Offer the upgrade in the post-purchase confirmation email.

95%+ accuracy on faded cursive handwriting. Gemini 2.5 Flash primary, Claude Sonnet fallback. Structured extraction of ingredients, instructions, categories — not just raw text.

🎨

Rich Design

Full WYSIWYG book designer with custom cover photos, title pages, color themes, and page layouts. Not a template picker — a real design tool that feels personal.

📖

Physical Output

Professional print-on-demand via Lulu. Hardcover, softcover, coil binding. No minimum orders. Ships directly to the customer or gift recipient.

Emotional Narrative Advantage

"Preserve Grandma's recipes" is a stronger story than any competitor tells. Heritage Cookbook and CreateMyCookbook feel utilitarian. KeptPages should own the emotional space — this isn't a tool, it's an act of love.
